### Known Chunking Techniques: 

1. **Fixed-Size Chunking (Token/Character-based)**

The simplest and most common baseline. Text is split into chunks of a fixed number of tokens, characters, or words (e.g., 512 tokens), often with overlap (10–20%) to preserve context across boundaries. It’s fast, predictable, and works well for homogeneous, simple documents like short articles or FAQs. Widely used as a starting point due to its low computational cost.

2. **Recursive Chunking (Recursive Character/Text Splitter)**

A hierarchical approach that tries to split on meaningful separators in order (e.g., \n\n for paragraphs, then \n, sentences, words). It respects document structure better than fixed-size while ensuring chunks stay under a size limit. This is one of the most popular default strategies in production because it balances simplicity and coherence.

3. **Document Structure / Content-Aware Chunking**

Splits based on natural document boundaries such as pages, sections, headings, Markdown/HTML tags, or paragraphs. Includes page-level chunking (strong performer in NVIDIA benchmarks) and format-specific splitters (PDF, Markdown, code). Excellent for preserving logical units in structured documents like reports, manuals, or books.

4. **Semantic Chunking**

Uses embeddings to group sentences or small passages that are semantically similar, splitting at points where topic similarity drops (e.g., via cosine distance thresholds). Introduced/popularized by Greg Kamradt; now widely available in frameworks. It creates more meaningful, coherent chunks than purely syntactic methods and improves retrieval quality for narrative or technical content.

5. **Parent-Child / Hierarchical (Small-to-Big) Chunking**

Creates smaller “child” chunks for precise retrieval and links them to larger “parent” chunks (or full sections) that provide broader context to the LLM. This hybrid approach is very popular in production for balancing retrieval precision with sufficient context.

### Experimental / Emerging Chunking Techniques: 

1. **Late Chunking**

Embeds the full document (or large sections) first, then chunks the resulting embeddings. This preserves global context better than chunk-then-embed approaches. Recent papers evaluate it favorably for maintaining coherence in long documents.

2. **Contextual Retrieval / Context-Enriched Chunking**

Augments each chunk with additional surrounding context, summaries, or metadata generated by an LLM or other means before embedding. Aims to solve the “lost in the middle” and decontextualization problems.

3. **Agentic / LLM Driven Chunking**

Uses an LLM itself to intelligently decide chunk boundaries based on content, task, or instructions (e.g., “split into self-contained reasoning units”). More computationally expensive but adaptive to complex or domain-specific documents.

4. **Growing Window / Adaptive Semantic Chunking**

Variants of semantic chunking that use dynamic windows or growing boundaries to better handle weak semantic transitions. Proposed in recent research to address limitations of standard semantic splitting.

5. **Recursive Semantic / Hybrid Chunking**

Combines recursive structural splitting with semantic similarity checks at each level. Other hybrids include modality-specific (for multimodal docs) or task-optimized chunking. These are actively researched for better performance on specialized corpora like legal, scientific, or academic papers.
